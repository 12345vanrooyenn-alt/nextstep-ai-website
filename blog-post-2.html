DM Automation: How to Reply to 200 Instagram DMs a Day Without Losing the Human Feel

Most DM automation feels robotic. Here's how we build systems that sound like you — and convert.

Caleb van Rooyen
· 6 min read · March 2026

A clothing brand with a decent Instagram following gets flooded with DMs every single day. "Is this in stock?" "What's the size guide?" "Do you ship to Australia?" "Can I get this in black?" It's relentless — and it never stops when you're running a drop-based or seasonal brand.

The owner has two choices: ignore the DMs and lose sales, or spend three hours a day copy-pasting the same answers. Most brands pick one of those two bad options. We built a third one.

The problem isn't volume. It's that volume, without a system, becomes a tax on your time. And time is the one thing a founder never has enough of.

Why Most DM Automation Fails

Brands that have tried DM automation before usually come to us frustrated. They set up a basic bot, it sent the same canned response to everyone, and customers either ignored it or got annoyed. Here's why that happens every time:

  • Canned responses feel robotic. "Thanks for reaching out! We'll get back to you soon." Nobody believes a brand that responds to "Is the hoodie in XL?" with a generic holding message. The sale is gone before it starts.
  • Generic chatbots can't handle product-specific questions. A basic bot can answer "what are your shipping times" — but it falls apart the moment someone asks about a specific colourway, a sizing comparison between two products, or whether a sold-out item is coming back.
  • Customers can tell — and they leave. When a response feels automated, it signals that you don't care. For a clothing brand where the relationship between brand and customer is everything, that's a trust killer.

The fix isn't more automation. It's smarter automation — the kind that knows the difference between a purchase signal and a complaint, and responds to each accordingly.

The Nextstep AI DM Framework

We built our DM system in three stages. Each one handles a different piece of the problem:

1
Step 1: Intent Detection
Every incoming DM is classified into a bucket before a single word is written in reply. Buckets include: product question, order status, size guide request, complaint, and — most importantly — purchase signal. A customer saying "how does this fit on a size 10?" is a buying signal, not a support ticket. The system knows the difference.
2
Step 2: GPT-4o Dynamic Reply
No templates. No fill-in-the-blank. Each reply is written fresh by GPT-4o with the brand's tone, product catalogue, and FAQ injected as context. The result reads like the owner wrote it — specific, helpful, and on-brand. A size query gets a size answer. A stock question gets an accurate stock answer. A purchase signal gets a reply that nudges toward checkout.
3
Step 3: Escalation Logic
Not every DM should be automated. Complaints, refund requests, and anything the system isn't confident about get flagged immediately — a Telegram alert fires to the owner within seconds, with the full DM thread context attached. The owner sees it, handles it, and the customer never waits more than a few minutes for a human response on the things that matter most.

What This Looks Like in Numbers

The time savings alone justify the system — but the conversion data is where it gets interesting. Size queries in particular convert at a significantly higher rate when they're answered within seconds rather than hours.

8 hours per week saved.
34% reply-to-purchase conversion on size queries — compared to under 10% when those same queries were handled manually the next day.

The eight hours is straightforward — that's manual DM time eliminated. The 34% conversion rate is the part that surprises most brand owners. It turns out that answering a size question immediately, in a helpful and specific way, while the customer is still in the mood to buy, is dramatically more effective than answering it six hours later when they've already moved on.

The Human Feel Principle

The most common objection we hear: "won't people know it's a bot?" The short answer is no — because we don't build bots. We build brand voice extensions.

It takes about five seconds longer to feel human than it does to feel robotic. A robotic response is generic, fast, and clearly templated. A human response is specific, warm, and sounds like it was written for this person, about this question, right now.

Our system is trained on three things before it goes live: the brand's own past DM conversation history, the full product catalogue with descriptions and sizing details, and an FAQ built by the founder in their own words. The result is a system that sounds like the owner wrote it — because in a very real sense, it was. The owner's voice is the training data.

We've had clients tell us customers responded with "thanks so much, you're so helpful" — to a message the system wrote. That's the standard we build to.
