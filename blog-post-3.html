Email Automation

Why Most Klaviyo Flows Fail (And What We Do Differently for Clothing Brands)

Caleb van Rooyen
· 7 min read · March 2026

Klaviyo is the most powerful email tool most clothing brands have — and the most underused. The average brand sets up a cart abandonment flow once, never touches it again, and wonders why they're only seeing 8–12% recovery. The ceiling is 35–40%. Here's why they're leaving that gap open.

The 3 Failure Points We See in Every Audit

  1. Timing is wrong. Most brands send email #1 at 30 minutes — too early. The optimal window is 1 hour. Email 2 at 24 hours, email 3 at 72 hours.
  2. Generic copy. "You left something behind 👀" is tired. A subject line with the customer's first name and the actual product name outperforms generic every time.
  3. No segmentation. A VIP customer who's bought 5 times should never see the same message as a first-time visitor.

The Nextstep AI Flow Architecture

Here's what we build instead.

1
Email 1 — AI-personalised subject line
GPT-4o writes a custom subject line using the exact product name and customer first name. Body is short — 3 sentences. No fluff.
1 hour after abandonment
2
Email 2 — Social proof injection
Pulls a review for the specific product left in cart. If no review exists, defaults to a brand testimonial. Still personalised to the item.
24 hours after abandonment
3
Email 3 — Urgency or soft offer
If low stock: says so. If not: soft discount offer, auto-expires in 48 hours. No evergreen fake urgency.
72 hours after abandonment
SMS
SMS — Short, direct, single link (optional)
No images. No fluff. Just a personalised one-liner with a direct checkout link. Converts at 2× email on mobile.
4 hours after abandonment

Why Clothing Brands Specifically?

  • High AOV. At £80–£250 per order, even 5 recovered carts per month is significant revenue — the math compounds fast.
  • Visual products. Email design and product imagery matter more in fashion than almost any other niche. A well-built flow shows the product, not just a link.
  • Seasonal buying patterns. Flows need to adapt to product drops, sales events, and restocks automatically — static templates can't keep up.
